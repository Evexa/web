In order to at least approximately estimate the number of stars in space, you should know that, for example, in our star galaxy there are approximately 150 billion stars, and in the entire Universe the number of galaxies, according to the same scientists, is several billion. At the same time, terrestrial astronomers have long considered the stars visible in the sky with a simple, naked eye - there are only about 6 thousand of them. All these stars are described, long studied and even listed in special cosmic star catalogs.

Looking carefully at the sky, it is easy to notice that some stars in space seem larger or brighter than others. The main version of the classification of stars was introduced by astronomers in the II century BC, it was developed by the ancient Greek scientist Hipparchus of Nicaea. This method of classifying stars in space is to divide the celestial bodies into groups depending on their size. However, by the word "magnitude" it is necessary to understand not the true sizes of stars in space, but their brightness. According to the classifier, the brightest stars in space are stars of the first magnitude. They shine 2.5 times brighter than stars of the second magnitude, and they naturally 2.5 times brighter than stars of the third magnitude, etc. By the way, with the naked eye, without a telescope, it is possible to make out stars up to the sixth magnitude.

Remember that in Greek, the word planet means "wandering star." Indeed, planets and stars are very similar. However, if you look at them more closely, you will notice that the stars flicker, and the planets shine with an even calm light. This is because the stars themselves emit light, and the planet only reflects the light falling on its surface. In addition, the planets are constantly moving around the horizon, wandering or floating in the common people, not taking a certain place in the sky. Because the planets are floating around the horizon, revolving around their star, they are called the planet or the planning star.
